A co-operative training experiment · Windows · free

Train a language model, together.

Prism Studio is a live, co-operative training experiment, not a finished model yet. It is a from-scratch language model that learns from the people running it: you train your own copy on your own machine, on the CPU or your NVIDIA GPU, with no PyTorch and no CUDA toolkit, and it can join a shared colony where everyone's copies teach each other. Today it is small and its replies are rough. If you would like to shape its personality, feed it, or lend it compute, come join in.

What you can do

One window: teach it, talk to it, network it.

Everything is a training pair. Drop text into a folder, press Train, and watch it learn live. Then type at it, or connect it to other machines running the same thing.

Model

Train and chat

Train from a folder of plain text and prompt→target files, with a live log of loss, speed and the samples it is generating. Talk to it in a REPL. Save, load, or reset the model to a blank slate whenever you want.

Network

A colony that learns together

Optionally join a peer colony: nodes teach each other by trading training examples and averaging tiny slices of their weights. Group-chat several models at once. This is the research idea, many whole models cohering, made visible.

Sub-node

Run a second cell

Spin up a second independent model in the same window, with its own data and memory, that also joins the network. A little colony on one machine, so you can watch two learners diverge and converge.

Why it's interesting

The mechanism, not the fluency.

Prism is a from-scratch transformer built on a few unusual ideas. It will not out-talk a real chatbot. It does some things a normal small model cannot.

Numbers as phasors

Arithmetic it was never taught

Numbers are stored as decodable patterns, so the model computes on operands it has never seen rather than memorising. In our benchmark it reads held-out arithmetic at 52%, against 16% for a same-size ordinary transformer.

Mergeable gradients

Mismatched machines, one model

Gradients merge to the bit, so a slow laptop and a fast desktop contribute identical results. That is what lets a colony of ordinary machines train one coherent model, with every node holding the whole thing.

Your hardware

CPU, or your GPU

The whole thing is plain C# from scratch, no PyTorch or TensorFlow. It trains on the CPU you already have, and automatically accelerates on your NVIDIA GPU when one is present, through your existing driver, with no CUDA toolkit to install.

How to teach it

It becomes what you say to it.

The model starts blank, with no personality. It becomes whatever it is trained on, and it only ever learns from what real people say, never from its own output. The voice it grows is the voice you give it.

It learns your voice

Every message trains it

Each thing you type in the REPL is saved and trained as "the conversation so far → your reply". Talk to it about what you care about, in your own voice, and it drifts toward sounding like you. On the network, it also picks up the human turns of the people you are connected to.

Only humans count

It never learns from a machine

It only ever trains toward real human turns, never toward text a model generated, its own or a peer's. That is deliberate: its personality is made only of real people's words, so it cannot spiral into its own nonsense.

Change its training data

Press Open data folder in the app (or browse to %LOCALAPPDATA%\Prism\data). Drop files into any of the lanes:

  • text\ — plain .txt files. It learns to continue writing like them: a corpus, a style, an author.
  • pairs\ — tab-separated lines, prompt<TAB>target, one per line. It learns to answer these, like flash-card Q&A.
  • chat\ — your REPL conversations, filled in automatically as you talk. This is where your own voice lands.

Then press Train to fold the new data in, and Reset model to wipe the weights and start over. Your data is kept either way, resetting only clears what it has learned, so you retrain to rebuild.

How training works

  • Every lane gets an equal share of each training step, so a handful of pairs lines shape it as much as a giant text file. Small, deliberate data has real pull.
  • An epoch is a length of time, not a pass over the data. It trains on random draws for the minutes you give it, the corpus is far too big to read in full.
  • Numbers are frozen. The codec that lets it do arithmetic is pinned, so teaching it new words never breaks its math.
  • Nothing is required. Add as little or as much as you like; an empty folder just means it has nothing to learn from yet.
Know before you run

It's an experiment, and it talks to the internet.

It joins a public colony

Read this first

On launch, Prism Studio connects to a public colony and shares training examples and small slices of its model weights with other people running it. That is the colony experiment working as intended. But it means anything you put in the training folder can be sent to strangers. Do not train it on anything private.

Expectations

A toy, honestly

This is a training experiment, not a working model yet. It is small and character-level, so replies are short and often nonsense, closer to a talking parrot than an assistant. Large-scale colony training is unproven; it runs today at small scale. It is offered as-is, a shared experiment to join, not a product with support.

Join in

Add yourself to the experiment.

Free, and a single self-contained app: unzip anywhere and run, no install and no .NET needed. Once it is running, there are three ways to contribute:

  • Personality — chat with your copy, and it learns to talk like you.
  • Training — add text or question-and-answer pairs to its data folder.
  • Compute — leave it running in the colony, and your machine helps train the shared model.

How to run it

The app is not code-signed, so Windows SmartScreen will warn you. It runs fine; here is the safe way through:

  1. Unzip the folder somewhere (keep PrismStudio.exe next to its data folder).
  2. Right-click PrismStudio.exe → Properties → tick Unblock → OK.
  3. Double-click it. If a blue "Windows protected your PC" box appears, click More info → Run anyway.

You do not need to run it as administrator. If your antivirus quarantines it, that is a false positive on an unsigned app; allow it or skip the download.

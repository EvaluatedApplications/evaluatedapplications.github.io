Research · open access · reproducible

Arithmetic as algebra, and gradients that merge to the bit.

PrismFormer is our own research: a transformer where every token, word or number, is a bundle of unit complex numbers, and arithmetic falls out of the algebra rather than being memorised. Because its gradients merge exactly, a colony of ordinary CPUs can train one coherent model. The full paper, the code, and a runnable verification kit are open access.

Chen, D. S. (2026). A Self-Replicating Swarm of Tiny AIs: Phasor-Face Transformers (PrismFormer) with Arithmetic as Algebra and Bit-Exact, Mergeable Gradients. Zenodo. DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21384525

The idea

Let the representation carry the reasoning.

The starting question was simple: if you want a model to reason rather than recall, what would you actually have to represent? The answer that kept falling out was a codec, a fixed way to write any concept down so that one operation composes two of them and the answer can be read back.

Numbers as phasors

Arithmetic, not memory

Every token is a phasor face: a bundle of unit complex numbers composed by one operation, a complex multiply. Numbers are written so that this bind adds on one frequency band and multiplies on another, and the result is decoded by correlation. The arithmetic is in the algebra; it emerges from the codec with no training at all.

Legible working

You can read the answer out

Because a computed value is decodable rather than merely reachable, the model's working is legible one step at a time, column by column, with no trained probe bolted on to interpret it. The structure does the reasoning; the network only learns which moves to make.

Mergeable gradients

Mismatched machines, one model

The substrate can be read during backprop, so gradients accumulate into a detached buffer. A minibatch split into shards and summed is bit-for-bit identical to the serial result. That is what lets a full-replication swarm of plain CPUs train one model that stays coherent across mismatched hardware, where sharded GPU systems need matched accelerators.

Results, honestly

Every number against a fair baseline.

Each result is measured against a parameter-matched dense transformer at the same setting, and we state plainly what does not work yet.

  • Arithmetic with no training. Binding two number faces decodes straight to their exact sum and product, 100/100 and 81/81 single-digit pairs exact, before any gradient step.
  • Generalises to unseen operands. Given the same worked, column-by-column problem, it learns multi-digit add, subtract, and multiply/divide and generalises to operand pairs it never saw (held-out add 54.9%, subtract 70.0%, multiply 27.7%, divide 33.2%), where a matched transformer given the identical problem stays near zero.
  • Better relational generalisation. On comparison and relational tasks it generalises better than a transformer that fits the same training data, 65.9% vs 49.1% held-out on the fair tasks (both reach 100% on copy).
  • A competitive language model at size. 41.0% vs 21.7% next-character accuracy against the matched transformer.
  • Remembers more, for its size. Pound for pound it holds more than twice as many facts as a standard model the same size, and keeps working smoothly where the standard model becomes unstable and falls apart.
  • What is still open. Out-of-range extrapolation, relational reverse-inference, and large-scale convergence of the full colony remain unsolved. The paper includes a two-sided ablation that argues against part of our own central design choice in isolation, and says exactly when it holds and when it does not.
Reproducible

Re-run every result yourself.

Every quantitative claim is produced by a committed script. The exact source, the benchmarks, and a runnable verification kit are archived as a frozen snapshot alongside the paper, so the numbers are not something to take on trust, they are something to re-run in seconds.
